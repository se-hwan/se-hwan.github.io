Triple Junction Plasma Current Enhancement

Description/Motivation

I've been working on this project with Dr. Go since May of 2019 with funding from the NDnano fellowship. Building off the work of Dr. Michael Johnson, a PhD graduate from Notre Dame, I recreated his experimental rig that heated a pyroelectric crystal orthogonal to a needle counter-electrode. The needle was connected to a picoammeter that recorded the current of the generated plasma, and the data was collected using LabView. Taking long-exposure images of the needle tip, I found similar visual results to what Dr. Johnson reported (which is the picture above), and the measured currents matched his results as well.

From there, I explored strategies to enhance the resultant plasma current from the crystal. I tested various crystals, counter-electrodes, and surface modifications, and by utilizing what were known as "triple junctions" in the plasma science literature (the intersection of a dielectric, gas, and metal), I was able to enhance the current by an order of 10 by applying silver paint to the surface of the crystal. We found that the geometry of the applied paint would significantly affect the resultant current, with a sharper angle resulting in a higher current. These empirical results were corroborated with COMSOL modeling. The crystal, silver paint, and plate counter-electrode were modeled in 2D, and a significant enhancement of the electric field was seen at the triple junction that corresponded with literature values for the breakdown of air.

The currents obtained are on the order of nanoamps, which would be difficult to translate into any significant application. In terms of research however, there are strong signs that simple surface modifications can enhance plasma current from these dielectric crystals. This setup could easily be applied to a piezoelectric controlled more directly by mechanical vibration, which does have practical and industrial uses. Being able to control and enhance the resultant current from a different crystal may be viable with further work.

Status

Ongoing. Currently working on moving my testing rig into a vacuum chamber and modeling the system in 3D in COMSOL to further confirm results and determine if pyroelectric electron emission is observed.

Clock Angles

Description/Motivation

At some point, I was sitting through lecture for a class I absolutely hated, and to maintain some semblance of sentience and attention, I focused on the analog clock that was in the room waiting for the time to tick by. Desperately bored, I had this random thought - when would the minute and hour hand be straight? Of course, the obvious answers would be at noon and at 6:00, but I realized that there would be many, many other times as well when this would happen. Remembering that I had learned how to use rotation matrices in the past few weeks, I realized I could easily write a program to determine the necessary times. If I represented the position of the hour and minute hand as vectors, and multiplied them by the rotation matrix at discrete intervals, the dot product of their positions would give me information regarding their linearity. Because I assumed a discretely ticking analog clock, I had to account for some wiggle room in the orthogonality condition, but it was still fairly accurate. I think I had so much fun with this project because, in a very, very small way, I had a question new to me (I'm sure this has been done before, but I did not look at any references), thought about how I'd go about answering it, and found a solution. No textbook, no Chegg, no outside help - I used the mathematical tools I had learned and applied them independently, and it felt incredibly satisfying. Now I have a new piece of trivia I can use to impress people at parties.

Status

Complete
Solutions:
The clock hands will be linear at 12:00, 12:33, 1:05, 1:38, 2:11, 2:44, 3:16, 3:49, 4:22, 4:55, 5:27, 6:00, 6:33, 7:05, 7:38, 8:11, 8:44, 9:16, 9:49, 10:22, 10:55, 11:27. The clock hands are orthogonal at 12:16, 12:50, 1:20, 1:54, 2:27, 3:00, 3:32, 4:05, 4:38, 5:10, 5:43, 6:16, 6:49, 7:21, 7:54, 8:27, 9:00, 9:32, 10:05, 10:38, 11:10, 11:43.

Monopoly Simulation

Description/Motivation

Another one of my curiosity projects. I have this bad habit of wanting to optimize the games I play instead of just enjoying them. I was curious about which tiles in Monopoly people would land on most, and more interestingly, which of those were propertied. Of course, with the number of turns played in an average monopoly game (answers seem to vary, but a few forums seem to say between 30-45 turns is about right) compared to my simulation runs (1000+), I doubt this would give me the edge over any game I actually played (putting my personal skill at Monopoly aside), but it was still a fun thought experiment.

Status

Complete.
The most common tile landed on is, pretty intuitively, the jail tile (since there are two tiles that will place a player there). The second most common is the chance tile 7 tiles away from that jail cell, and the tile most landed upon that was propertied is Tenessee Avenue. Of course, it should be noted that I did not take into account all the other rules of Monopoly (from the effects of chance cards, being sent to jail, etc.), and this is strictly based on tolls alone. A more complete study of Monopoly can be found here. Taking those into account, Illinois Avenue should be every player's first buy, but at the end of the day, the difference is so small that it would hardly make a difference for a regular game.

Nuclear Reactor Design Project

Description/Motivation

For a nuclear science elective I took, the final project involved designing a nuclear reactor with the intent of creating some target nuclei through fission or decay, and estimate the cost and profit (disregarding things like infrastructure, power, etc.) from the enterprise. I chose Europium because of how rare and valuable it was, but as it turns out, the idea probably wouldn't hold up if proposed in Shark Tank. As a whole, I still feel like the chemistry and physics involved at atomic levels is too abstract and uncomfortable for me. I enjoyed the class, but it was hard for me to marry the derived equations with their physical components. An equation like F=ma just seems so natural and logical, because I can imagine a block being pushed on a frictionless surface. It's harder to do the same with a spinning electron following some crazy random pattern in a loosely defined electric field.

Status
